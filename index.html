The Real Problem

Not about aesthetics.
About time and lost signal.

Every week, a buyer lands on the pricing page and manually adds up airframe, engine, and options with no running total — then sends an email asking for a quote that gets calculated by hand. That is time better spent building aircraft.

Every month, a serious buyer arrives on a site that predates their smartphone and quietly wonders if the operation is still active. They do not always call to find out. Some of them just leave.

And the fleet — hundreds of Aerolite 103s flying across the US, Canada, Australia — is invisible to every first-time visitor. There is no owner count, no community, no proof that hundreds of people before them already made this decision. That social proof exists. It just has no home.

The Work

Three phases.
Each one standalone.

Nothing is bundled. Each phase is a complete, usable upgrade on its own. The pace and scope are entirely your call.

Phase 01
A Site That Reflects What the Aerolite Has Earned

A clean, fast, mobile-responsive rebuild structured around how buyers actually move through the information. Grand Champion history, fleet reach, owner community — all visible before the first phone call. fly103.com consolidated so no visitor gets lost between two identical sites. The builder who spent 1,000 hours mastering aircraft construction before selling a single one deserves a page that says so — because a $30,000 buyer needs to know who built what they're about to fly.

A buyer who Googles "Aerolite 103" arrives somewhere that matches the aircraft's actual reputation.
Phase 02
A Configurator That Quotes So the Factory Doesn't Have To

Exactly what the demo above shows — live on the site, connected to the factory. Buyer selects airframe, engine, color, add-ons. Watches the total update in real time. Submits a complete spec sheet directly to DeLand. No back-and-forth. No manual arithmetic.

The most repetitive part of the sales process runs itself.
Phase 03
A Community Presence Worth Having

An owner gallery, build stories, a structured way for the fleet to feed back into the brand. The Aerolite community is active — on forums, Facebook, at Oshkosh, across three countries. It has no home on the domain it should belong to. First-time visitors need to see that others made this decision before them.

Social proof that lives on your domain, not on platforms you don't control.
Why Now

The market is moving.
The Aerolite is already positioned for it.

The Part 103 ultralight segment is growing. Factory-built, ready-to-fly models are the fastest-growing category as buyers move away from long self-build timelines. The Aerolite 103 — quick-build, Grand Champion pedigree, priced below almost everything comparable — is exactly what this market wants right now. The digital presence is the only thing not keeping pace.

The aircraft already has international reach — dealers in Canada and Australia distribute it independently. The demand exists beyond Florida. The website doesn't reflect it yet.
